Understanding Your Treatment Options

What Is Glioblastoma?

Glioblastoma (GBM) is an aggressive brain tumor that requires urgent, intensive treatment. Standard care combines surgery, radiation, and chemotherapy. While these therapies target the tumor, they can also affect healthy brain tissue—particularly the networks responsible for memory, language, and decision-making.

Until recently, there was no practical way to predict how different treatments would affect your cognitive function. That's changing.

Why Protecting Cognition Matters

Your brain's networks control memory, language, and thinking. Image: Unsplash/Robina Weermeijer

Your quality of life depends on more than tumor control. Preserving your ability to remember, communicate, and think clearly allows you to:

  • Maintain independence in daily activities
  • Continue meaningful relationships
  • Participate actively in treatment decisions
  • Plan for your future with clarity

Research shows that treatment-related cognitive decline is often preventable when care teams have the right information at the right time.

What Is Organoid Testing?

How It Works

Think of organoids as miniature models of your tumor grown in a laboratory from your own cells. These tiny "mini-brains" allow scientists to test different drugs and see which ones work best against your specific tumor.

How brain organoids develop from stem cells. Source: Nature Cell Death & Differentiation (2020)

What This Means for You

  • Personalized treatment: Doctors can test multiple drugs on your organoid before choosing the best one for you
  • Avoid ineffective drugs: Skip treatments unlikely to help your specific tumor
  • Faster decisions: Get results in weeks rather than months of trial and error

Recent studies from institutions like Weill Cornell Medicine have shown that organoid models successfully predict how patients respond to treatment, making them a powerful tool for personalizing your care.

What Is Connectome Mapping?

How It Works

Your brain is like a complex highway system, with billions of connections linking different regions. Some areas act as major hubs—like central stations—coordinating memory, language, and thinking. A connectome scan creates a map showing exactly where these important hubs are located.

Brain networks visualized: white matter connections coordinate memory and language. Image: Unsplash

The scan uses special MRI techniques that track how different brain regions communicate. This information helps your surgical team plan the safest approach and helps radiation oncologists avoid critical areas.

What This Means for You

  • Personalized surgical planning: Surgeons can avoid damage to your critical memory and language centers
  • Optimized radiation: Radiation fields shaped to protect vulnerable networks
  • Better predictions: Doctors can estimate cognitive risks before treatment starts
  • Long-term quality of life: Reduced risk of preventable cognitive problems

Research from major medical centers has demonstrated that connectome mapping can identify brain network disruptions invisible on standard MRI scans, helping predict treatment outcomes and preserve cognitive function.

How These Tools Work Together

Organoid testing tells your doctor which drugs will fight your tumor most effectively.

Connectome mapping tells your doctor which treatments pose the lowest risk to your memory and thinking abilities.

Together, they form a complete picture: the right treatment for your tumor, chosen with your brain's health in mind.

Doctors use advanced imaging to plan safer treatments. Image: Unsplash/National Cancer Institute

Questions to Ask Your Doctor

Before Surgery:

  • Can we order a connectome scan to map my memory and language networks?
  • How will the surgical approach account for cognitive-risk areas?
  • Which brain networks are most vulnerable in my case?

Before Starting Chemotherapy or Targeted Therapy:

  • Is organoid testing available to identify which drugs my tumor is sensitive to?
  • Can we cross-reference drug options with my connectome map?
  • How long does organoid testing take?

During Treatment:

  • How will my cognitive function be monitored over time?
  • Are there alternative therapies if my current regimen shows cognitive impact?
  • What early warning signs should I watch for?

For Clinical Trials:

  • Does this trial incorporate organoid or connectome data?
  • How does the trial protocol account for cognitive outcomes?

You Have the Right to Ask

Organoid testing and connectome mapping are evidence-based tools backed by research from institutions like Weill Cornell Medicine, Oxford University, and The Lancet Neurology. Forward-thinking cancer centers are beginning to integrate these approaches into standard care.

Bringing these options to your care team's attention is not just reasonable—it's an important part of advocating for the best possible treatment.

Clinical Integration of Organoid Profiling & Connectome Mapping

Evidence-based tools for cognition-preserving precision neuro-oncology

The Clinical Challenge

Standard GBM protocols achieve modest survival gains but frequently cause significant treatment-related cognitive morbidity. Chemotherapy, radiation, and surgical corridors disrupt memory, language, and executive networks—deficits that persist long after oncologic intervention and profoundly impact quality of life.

Conventional imaging cannot predict patient-specific drug sensitivity or identify which neural circuits are most vulnerable to therapy. Treatment planning remains largely tumor-centric, with cognitive outcomes addressed reactively.

Organoid Drug-Response Profiling

Patient-derived GBM organoids show heterogeneous TMZ sensitivity. Source: Cell Reports (2019) - Linkous et al., Weill Cornell Medicine

Methodology

Patient-derived organoids generated from surgical specimens or iPSCs recapitulate tumor microenvironment and cellular heterogeneity. Systematic drug-panel testing includes:

  • Standard-of-care agents: temozolomide, lomustine, carmustine
  • Targeted therapies: EGFR inhibitors, VEGF inhibitors, mTOR inhibitors, MEK inhibitors
  • Salvage regimens: bevacizumab, tumor-treating fields, immunotherapy combinations
  • Novel compounds: identified through high-throughput screening

Assays Performed

  • Viability assays (ATP, alamarBlue, live/dead staining)
  • Apoptosis markers (caspase-3, TUNEL, AO/PI staining)
  • Proliferation indices (Ki67, EdU incorporation)
  • Pathway activation (phospho-proteomics, Western blot)
  • Molecular profiling (MGMT, IDH, EGFR, PTEN, TP53)

Clinical Utility - Key Research Findings

Organoid assays reveal actionable heterogeneity beyond molecular profiling:

  • TMZ sensitivity does not consistently correlate with MGMT methylation status in organoid models, highlighting importance of functional testing
  • GBM organoids preserve patient-specific drug response patterns and maintain tumor heterogeneity across passages
  • Organoid drug screening enables identification of effective salvage therapies in treatment-resistant cases
  • High-throughput organoid screening allows simultaneous testing of combination therapies

Sources: npj Precision Oncology (2024), Nature Communications (2025), Cell Reports (2019), PMC publications

Connectome-Based Vulnerability Mapping

Structural connectome analysis workflow using diffusion MRI. Source: Brain (Oxford, 2023)

FA color map showing relationship of tumor to white matter tracts. Source: Neurosurgical Focus (2020)

Methodology

Advanced MRI protocols characterize patient brain network architecture:

  • Diffusion MRI tractography: Probabilistic or deterministic fiber tracking maps structural connectivity and white matter pathways
  • Resting-state functional MRI: BOLD signal correlations identify functional networks and hub regions
  • Graph-theoretic analysis: Quantifies network topology, segregation, integration, and resilience
  • Network controllability modeling: Predicts cascade effects of local disruptions

Quantitative Metrics

  • Hub vulnerability scores (node degree, betweenness centrality)
  • Connectome disruption indices (deviation from age-matched controls)
  • Predicted cognitive impact scores for surgical corridors
  • Radiation field overlap with critical networks
  • Network reorganization capacity (plasticity potential)

Clinical Utility - Key Research Findings

Connectome analysis reveals widespread disruption and predicts outcomes:

  • Connectome disruptions extend beyond visible tumor margins and correlate with cognitive impairment (P < 0.001)
  • Distant connectome disruption predicts worse overall survival (HR = 1.46, P = 0.049) and progression-free survival (HR = 1.49, P = 0.019)
  • Preserved connectome regions show evidence of network reorganization associated with better outcomes
  • Structural connectivity mapping identifies occult tumor invasion invisible on conventional MRI
  • Functional proximity to tumor predicts network deterioration more accurately than anatomical distance

Sources: Brain (Oxford, 2023), The Lancet Neurology (2024), Neuro-Oncology Advances (2024), Scientific Reports (2020)

Integrated Treatment Planning Workflow

Phase 1: Pre-treatment Assessment (Weeks 1-6)

  1. Organoid establishment: Obtain surgical specimen or biopsy; initiate organoid culture (turnaround: 4-6 weeks)
  2. Drug panel screening: Test standard-of-care and targeted agents; generate sensitivity profiles
  3. Advanced MRI acquisition: Perform diffusion MRI (30-dir minimum, multi-shell preferred) and resting-state fMRI (8-10 minutes)
  4. Connectome analysis: Process tractography, generate network maps, calculate vulnerability indices

Phase 2: Multidisciplinary Integration (Week 6-7)

  1. Tumor board review: Neurosurgery, neuro-oncology, radiation oncology, neuroimaging, cognitive neurology
  2. Data synthesis: Cross-reference organoid sensitivity data with connectome risk maps
  3. Treatment optimization: Select surgical approach minimizing hub disruption; design radiation fields sparing vulnerable networks; choose systemic therapy based on organoid response and predicted cognitive impact

Phase 3: Treatment Execution & Monitoring (Ongoing)

  1. Baseline cognitive assessment: Montreal Cognitive Assessment, Hopkins Verbal Learning Test, Trail Making Test
  2. Treatment delivery: Execute optimized plan
  3. Longitudinal follow-up: Serial cognitive testing at 3, 6, and 12 months; repeat connectome imaging to assess network reorganization

Integrating Organoid–Connectome Workflows in Cancer Centers

A step-by-step framework for institutional adoption

Advanced MRI technology enables connectome mapping in clinical settings. Image: Unsplash

Why Institutions Should Adopt This Model

Strategic Value:

  • Clinical differentiation: Position your center as a leader in precision neuro-oncology
  • Quality metrics: Reduce preventable treatment-related cognitive morbidity (current rate: 33%)
  • Patient outcomes: Improve long-term functional outcomes and quality of life
  • Research productivity: Generate publishable data and secure competitive grant funding
  • Workforce development: Attract top faculty and trainees interested in cutting-edge approaches

Implementation Roadmap

Phase 1: Infrastructure Development (Months 1-3)

  • ☐ Identify organoid-testing capability (internal stem-cell core or external partner like Crown Bioscience, Vivia Biotech)
  • ☐ Upgrade MRI protocols: add 30+ direction diffusion sequence, 8-10 minute resting-state fMRI
  • ☐ Recruit computational expertise: neuroimaging physicist, network analyst, or collaboration with university partner
  • ☐ Secure seed funding: institutional innovation fund, pilot grant, or philanthropic support ($200K-500K typical)

Phase 2: Workflow Integration (Months 4-6)

  • ☐ Develop SOPs for tissue procurement, organoid culture, and drug screening
  • ☐ Establish connectome analysis pipeline using tools like FSL, MRtrix3, or DSI Studio
  • ☐ Train tumor board members through didactic sessions and case reviews
  • ☐ Implement cognitive assessment battery (MoCA, HVLT, TMT, COWAT)
  • ☐ Create data management system linking organoid results, imaging data, and outcomes

Phase 3: Pilot Cohort (Months 7-12)

  • ☐ Enroll 10-20 newly diagnosed GBM patients (power calculation for feasibility study)
  • ☐ Collect organoid sensitivity data and connectome maps prospectively
  • ☐ Integrate findings into multidisciplinary treatment planning
  • ☐ Track cognitive outcomes at 3, 6, and 12 months
  • ☐ Monitor feasibility metrics: completion rate, turnaround time, clinical impact

Phase 4: Program Expansion & Dissemination (Months 12+)

  • ☐ Analyze pilot data: cognitive outcome differences, survival trends, cost-benefit
  • ☐ Publish results in peer-reviewed journals (target: Neuro-Oncology, JCO, JAMA Oncology)
  • ☐ Present at national meetings (SNO, ASCO, ASTRO)
  • ☐ Expand to full clinical integration
  • ☐ Pursue R01/U01 funding for multi-center validation
  • ☐ Develop training program for other institutions

Required Infrastructure

Organoid Profiling:

  • Biosafety Level 2 laboratory space
  • Cell culture equipment (incubators, biosafety cabinets)
  • Microscopy (brightfield, fluorescence)
  • Viability assay instrumentation
  • Biobanking capability (liquid nitrogen storage)
  • Personnel: 1-2 FTE technicians/scientists

Connectome Mapping:

  • 3T MRI scanner (Siemens, GE, Philips)
  • Diffusion-capable gradients (≥40 mT/m)
  • Computational cluster or workstation
  • Analysis software (FSL, MRtrix3, FreeSurfer)
  • Visualization tools (Connectome Workbench, 3D Slicer)
  • Personnel: 0.5-1 FTE imaging physicist/analyst

Policy Recommendations

Institutional Level:

  • Mandate organoid profiling for all eligible newly diagnosed GBM cases
  • Include advanced connectome imaging in standard pre-surgical MRI bundles
  • Establish cognitive outcomes as tracked quality metric (analogous to surgical complications)
  • Create protected time for cognition-focused tumor board sessions

Payer & Regulatory Level:

  • Advocate for CPT codes specific to organoid drug-response testing
  • Pursue coverage for advanced connectome MRI under precision oncology indications
  • Support NIH BRAIN Initiative expansion to neuro-oncology applications
  • Engage FDA in regulatory pathways for organoid-guided therapy selection

Benefits of Adoption

  • Patient care: Reduces avoidable treatment-related cognitive decline
  • Precision medicine: Improves therapy selection accuracy
  • Quality of life: Enhances long-term functional outcomes without sacrificing tumor control
  • Institutional reputation: Establishes center as leader in next-generation neuro-oncology
  • Health equity: Extends personalized medicine to underserved populations
  • Research impact: Generates high-value publications and competitive grant proposals

Key Terms Glossary

Organoid: Three-dimensional tissue culture derived from stem cells that mimics organ structure and function.

Connectome: Comprehensive map of neural connections within the brain, visualized using diffusion and functional MRI.

Diffusion MRI: Imaging technique tracking water molecule movement to map white matter pathways.

Hub Vulnerability: Degree to which highly connected brain regions are susceptible to treatment damage.

MGMT Methylation: Molecular marker predicting temozolomide chemotherapy response.
